Glossary

The shared language for the (AI) Software Architect course. Every lesson uses these terms exactly.

Two families: the architect's craft (decisions, trade-offs, governance) and AI-systems concerns. Mixed deliberately — an AI architect needs both fluent.

Trade-off craft
An advantage paired with a disadvantage. The First Law of Software Architecture: everything is a trade-off. The architect's core job is analyzing them, not eliminating them.
Least-worst architecture craft
The goal is never the "best" architecture (it doesn't exist) but the one whose downsides you can most afford. Naming what you're giving up is the mark of an architect.
Architecture characteristic craft
A non-functional, system-wide quality the design must deliver — scalability, availability, security, evolvability, cost. Sometimes called the "-ilities." Derived from business needs, not chosen for taste. You can only optimize a few; choosing which is architecture.
Architect elevator craft
Gregor Hohpe's metaphor: the architect rides between the board room (strategy, money) and the engine room (code, systems), translating each to the other. Influence comes from connecting the floors, not living on one.
Influence without authority craft
Driving decisions you don't have the formal power to mandate — through trade-off clarity, business framing, and making others smarter. The defining skill separating architect from senior dev.
Selling options craft
Hohpe: architecture's product for the penthouse — the right, but not the obligation, to change direction later. Valuable precisely because the future is uncertain; priced in executive language ("costs X now, defers decision Y by 12 months").
First derivative craft
Hohpe: judge a system (and org) by its rate of change, not its position. The engine-room name for it is ETC; the penthouse name is strategic agility. Levers: toolchain, fast deploys, reversible decisions.
ADR — Architecture Decision Record craft
A short document capturing one significant decision: its context, the decision, and its consequences. Captures the why. Cheap to write, compounding to keep.
Fitness function craft
An automated check (often in CI) that assures an architectural decision stays true over time. "An ADR documents the decision; a fitness function enforces it." Catches drift before production.
C4 model craft
Hierarchical diagrams at four zoom levels — Context, Container, Component, Code. Shows the what; pairs with ADRs (the why) to form a navigable architecture knowledge graph.
Paved road craft
An approved, supported default path (patterns, tooling, guardrails) that makes the safe choice the easy choice. The architect's lever for scaling good decisions without reviewing every one.
RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation ai
Feeding an LLM relevant retrieved context at query time instead of baking knowledge into weights. The default pattern for grounding answers in your own data.
Fine-tuning ai
Adjusting a model's weights on task/domain data. Trades upfront cost & rigidity for behavior/style control. Architecturally: RAG vs fine-tune vs long-context is a classic trade-off decision.
Agentic / Adaptive RAG ai
Agentic: the LLM controls its own retrieval workflow (tools, multi-step). Adaptive: a router classifies each query by complexity and sends it to the cheapest strategy that will work — the pattern that makes production economically viable.
Plan-then-Execute ai
An agent architecture that plans the full task before acting, for predictability and resistance to control-flow hijacking. Recommended default for any non-trivial multi-tool agent.
Guardrails ai
Controls between user and model (and model and tools) that block unsafe input/output — hallucination, toxicity, injection, out-of-policy actions. An architectural concern, not a feature bolt-on.
Eval ai
Systematic measurement of an AI system's quality, safety, and regression — offline (datasets) and online (production). For AI systems, the eval+observability layer is load-bearing architecture, like tests.
Quality / cost / latency triangle ai
The AI architect's master trade-off, tracked continuously: better answers vs $ per call vs response time. Every AI design decision moves a point inside this triangle.
Complexity design
Ousterhout: the enemy — anything that makes a system hard to understand or change. Two symptoms: change amplification (one change touches many places) and cognitive load (you must know too much). Most design principles exist to fight it.
Deep / shallow module design
Deep: a simple interface over powerful, complex, hidden implementation — max value, min cost. Shallow: a fat interface for little functionality. Prefer deep modules.
Information hiding design
Burying implementation complexity so callers see only an abstraction. The mechanism that makes a module deep; also cuts inter-module dependencies.
ETC — Easy To Change design
The Pragmatic Programmer's unifying value: good design is design that's easy to change. Nearly every other tip is a special case of ETC.
DRY — Don't Repeat Yourself design
Every piece of knowledge has one single, authoritative representation. The risk isn't typing twice — it's forgetting to update a copy.
Orthogonality design
Independence between components: a change in one doesn't ripple to others. Achieved by "shy" code that does one job and ignores the rest. Enables isolated change, test, and reuse.
Tracer bullet design
A thin but real, end-to-end slice through every layer — kept and grown, not thrown away like a prototype. You aim by seeing where it lands.
Broken windows design
Fix small visible flaws fast; unrepaired neglect signals "nobody cares" and accelerates decay across the whole codebase.
