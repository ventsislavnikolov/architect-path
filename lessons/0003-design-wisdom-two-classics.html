Lesson 03 · Design Judgment

Design Wisdom: Two Classics

The vocabulary of complexity, depth, and change — from Ousterhout and the Pragmatic Programmers.

Why this, now. "Less coding, more design" is one of your drivers. An architect's edge isn't syntax — it's design judgment: seeing complexity before it metastasizes, and shaping modules that stay easy to change. These two books are the canon. This lesson builds the recall so the principles fire in the moment — in a design review, or defending an ADR.

Book 1 — A Philosophy of Software Design

John Ousterhout · Stanford

One thesis: complexity is the enemy, and nearly every other rule exists to manage it.1 Complexity is anything that makes a system hard to understand or change. It shows up as two symptoms: change amplification (a simple change touches many places) and cognitive load (you must hold too much in your head).

His central tool is the deep module: a simple interface over a powerful, complex implementation. The interface is the cost you pay; the functionality is the value you get. A shallow module inverts that — a fat interface for little payoff. The mechanism that makes modules deep is information hiding: bury the complexity so callers see only an abstraction.

Book 2 — The Pragmatic Programmer

Andrew Hunt & David Thomas

Its unifying value is ETC — Easy To Change: good design is design that's easy to change; every other tip is a special case.2

DRY

Every piece of knowledge has one single, authoritative representation. The enemy isn't typing twice — it's forgetting to update all copies.

Orthogonality

Independence: a change in one thing doesn't ripple to others. Write "shy" code that does one job and ignores the rest.

Tracer bullets

A thin but real, end-to-end slice through every layer. Unlike a prototype, it's kept and grown — you aim by seeing where it lands.

Broken windows

Fix small visible flaws fast. Left alone, visible neglect signals "nobody cares" and decay accelerates across the codebase.

Both books converge on one architect instinct: manage complexity so the system stays easy to change — deep modules that hide it, orthogonal parts that contain it, and single sources of truth that stop it multiplying.

Do this now — your tangible win

Pick one module or service you own. In three sentences:

  1. Is it deep or shallow? (Compare its interface size to the functionality it delivers.)
  2. Name one bit of complexity it leaks to callers that it could hide.
  3. State one change that would make it easier to change (ETC) — deepen, orthogonalize, or de-duplicate.
