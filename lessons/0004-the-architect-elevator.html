The Architect Elevator

Riding between the engine room and the penthouse — where your artifacts become influence.

Why this, now. You have the artifacts: a trade-off sentence (L1) and an ADR (L2). But an internal promotion is granted by people who never read ADRs. This lesson is about carrying your reasoning up the building — the "broader influence" your mission names, done deliberately.

Gregor Hohpe's metaphor: a modern organization is a high-rise. Business strategy is set in the penthouse; software gets built in the engine room. In many companies the middle floors are occupied by steering committees and slide decks — and information degrades every floor it passes. The architect's defining act is to ride the elevator: move personally between floors and translate both directions, skipping the lossy middle.1

Your value as an architect isn't living on the top floor or being the smartest in the engine room — it's the number of floors you span, and making the people on each floor smarter rather than more impressed.

What each floor hears

The same decision — your React ADR — sounds different on each floor. Riding the elevator means switching vocabulary without switching the truth:

Penthouse

"We're standardizing our frontend technology so we can hire faster, reuse web skills for the mobile app, and reduce the risk of betting on a dying stack."

Middle floors

"One approved stack, a migration path for legacy apps, and a paved-road starter — teams keep autonomy inside those guardrails."

Engine room

"New frontends use the React starter (router, state, lint preset included). CI blocks new Angular/Ember packages — see ADR-0007."

Same decision, three renderings. Notice the penthouse version contains zero technology names except the one being standardized — it's all money, risk, and speed.

Selling options

What does the penthouse actually buy from architecture? Hohpe's answer: options — the right, but not the obligation, to change direction later.2 Good architecture keeps doors open: the React paved road is an option on mobile (React Native) that you may or may not exercise. Options have real value because the future is uncertain — and executives, who think in portfolios and hedges, understand this language natively. "This design costs 10% more now but lets us defer the vendor decision 12 months" is an architect sentence a CFO can price.

Living in the first derivative

Hohpe: what matters isn't your position but your rate of change — the first derivative. An architecture is good if it lets the organization change quickly: a tuned toolchain, fast deploys, reversible decisions.3 This links straight back to Lesson 3: ETC ("easy to change") is the engine-room name for the same value the penthouse calls "strategic agility". You now have the same principle in both floors' vocabulary — that is the elevator.

Do this now — your tangible win

Take your React ADR (or any decision you own) and write its penthouse rendering: ≤ 3 sentences, rules of the floor:

  1. No technology names except, at most, the thing being decided.
  2. Every sentence must carry money, risk, speed, or optionality.
  3. End with the option it buys: "this keeps the door open to ___".
